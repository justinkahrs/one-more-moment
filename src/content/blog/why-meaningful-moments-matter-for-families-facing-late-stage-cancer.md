A meaningful Moment is not only about the day itself. It is about what the person and their family are able to carry forward.

For an adult facing Stage IV, advanced, or metastatic cancer, time can begin to feel shaped by appointments, decisions, symptoms, paperwork, and uncertainty. Families often feel that weight too. Spouses and partners, children, parents, friends, and caregivers may all be trying to love someone well while also managing fear, logistics, and change.

One More Moment helps adults in Michigan facing Stage IV, advanced, or metastatic cancer create meaningful Moments with the people they love. These Moments are about time, connection, dignity, and memory. They do not remove grief, and they do not change the medical reality of cancer. But they can create space for something deeply human: being together on purpose.

## Late-Stage Cancer Affects the Whole Family

Late-stage cancer is never carried by one person alone.

The person facing cancer may be living with physical changes, emotional strain, treatment decisions, and the knowledge that time matters differently than it once did. At the same time, loved ones may be trying to offer support while facing their own sadness, worry, exhaustion, and practical responsibilities.

That circle can include:

- Spouses and partners
- Children
- Parents
- Siblings and extended family
- Friends
- Caregivers
- Neighbors, coworkers, faith communities, and others who care

A meaningful Moment can give that circle a way to gather around the person as a whole human being, not only around the diagnosis.

## Why Time Together Matters

When life is full of medical appointments, scans, phone calls, insurance questions, and uncertainty, something personal on the calendar can matter.

A Moment can give the person and their loved ones something to look forward to. It can create a pause from the constant planning and pressure. It can offer a chance to sit around a table, visit a favorite place, share a familiar tradition, or simply be together without cancer being the only subject in the room.

This kind of time does not have to be perfect. It does not need to erase sadness. Its value is often quieter than that.

It can help people say what they want to say. It can give family members a shared memory that is not defined only by illness. It can help everyone breathe for a little while.

## A Moment Does Not Have to Be Extravagant

The most meaningful Moment is not always the biggest one.

Sometimes a Moment is:

- A meal with family
- A visit to a favorite place
- Time with someone important
- A small trip
- A meaningful conversation
- A simple day surrounded by loved ones
- Help removing a practical barrier so people can be together

For one person, a meaningful Moment might be a ballgame, a lake visit, a short getaway, or a special event. For someone else, it may be a quiet afternoon at home with the people they love most.

What matters is not whether the Moment looks impressive from the outside. What matters is whether it feels meaningful to the person and the people who will carry that memory forward.

## Why Memories Matter for Families

Families remember details.

They remember conversations. They remember laughter. They remember where everyone sat, who was there, what the weather felt like, and the small things someone said that stayed with them later.

They remember a favorite restaurant, a familiar road, a song in the car, a hand held across a table, or the look on someone's face when a plan became real.

A Moment cannot take away the pain of losing someone. It should never be presented that way. But it can become part of a family's story. It can remind loved ones that, during a difficult season, something meaningful was still made possible.

For many families, those memories become a way to keep connection close.

## Dignity Beyond the Diagnosis

Late-stage cancer can make a person's world feel smaller. Medical routines, limitations, and urgent decisions can sometimes crowd out the parts of life that made someone feel most like themselves.

A meaningful Moment can help restore dignity by centering the person, not the disease.

It can honor what they love, who they love, where they feel connected, and what they still want to experience. It can give loved ones a chance to see them not only as a patient, but as a spouse, parent, child, friend, mentor, storyteller, neighbor, and individual with a life full of meaning.

That matters.

## Privacy Matters Too

Not every Moment needs to be public to be powerful.

Some Moments may be shared publicly only with permission from the person and family. Other Moments remain private, known only to the people who were there. Both can matter deeply.

One More Moment respects the privacy and dignity of every applicant, family, and referral partner. Personal and medical information should be shared carefully. You can review the organization's [Privacy Policy](/privacy-policy/) to learn more about how submitted information is used.

## How Donors and Supporters Help

Supporting a Moment is not only about funding an activity. It is about helping create time.

Donors and supporters can help remove barriers that may stand between a person and a meaningful experience with loved ones. That support may help with practical needs, coordination, travel, lodging, tickets, meals, accessibility, or other details connected to making a Moment possible.

Most of all, it can help give families something meaningful to carry forward.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Why do meaningful Moments matter for families facing late-stage cancer?

Meaningful Moments can give adults facing late-stage cancer and their loved ones time to be together in a personal way. They can create memories, support connection, and help families carry forward something meaningful from a difficult season.

### Does a Moment have to be a big trip or event?

No. A Moment can be simple. It might be a meal, a favorite place, a visit with someone important, a small trip, a meaningful conversation, or a quiet day surrounded by loved ones.

### Are all Moments shared publicly?

No. Some Moments may be shared publicly only with permission from the person and family. Many Moments remain private and still matter deeply.

### Can a donor help support a Moment?

Yes. Donors help make Moments possible by supporting the practical needs and coordination that can help adults facing late-stage cancer spend meaningful time with loved ones.

### How can someone request a Moment?

## About the Author

**Jason Pohlonski is the Founder and Executive Director of One More Moment**, a Michigan-based nonprofit created to help adults facing Stage IV, advanced, or metastatic cancer experience meaningful Moments with the people they love.

One More Moment was inspired by Jason's personal experience with his mother's cancer journey and the realization that, near the end of life, even a simple Moment together can mean everything. Through One More Moment, Jason and the organization work to provide dignity, connection, and meaningful experiences for adults and families navigating late-stage cancer.

## About One More Moment

**One More Moment** grants meaningful wish experiences for adults in Michigan who are facing Stage IV, advanced, or metastatic cancer and who lack the resources to create that Moment on their own.

Every request is reviewed with care. Eligibility may include Michigan residency, a qualifying cancer diagnosis, financial or logistical need, and confirmation from an oncology provider.
