Families often wonder when the right time is to request a wish for an adult with cancer. It can feel too soon. It can feel too emotional. It can feel like bringing up the idea means accepting something no one is ready to say out loud.

But a One More Moment request is not about giving up. It is about listening to what matters now.

One More Moment helps adults in Michigan facing Stage IV, advanced, or metastatic cancer create meaningful Moments with the people they love. A Moment may be simple or more involved, public or private, close to home or a short trip away. What matters most is that it centers the person facing cancer and gives them meaningful time with loved ones.

In many cases, earlier is better. Meaningful Moments take time to understand, plan, and coordinate. Families do not need to wait until the very end of life to begin a request.

For a broader overview of adult cancer wishes in Michigan, visit our guide to [wish granting for adults with cancer in Michigan](/adult-cancer-wishes-michigan/).

## Why Earlier Requests Can Help

An adult cancer wish often begins with a simple idea: someone they want to see, somewhere they want to go, a meal they want to share, or a day they want to spend away from the pressure of appointments and logistics.

Starting earlier gives One More Moment more room to understand what would feel meaningful, comfortable, and realistic.

Earlier requests can help because they allow:

- More time to understand what matters most to the person facing cancer
- More time to coordinate family, travel, meals, lodging, or local experiences
- More flexibility around the person's energy, comfort, privacy, and changing needs
- More ability to involve donors, volunteers, or local partners when appropriate

Earlier does not mean rushed. It means there is more space to plan thoughtfully.

One More Moment does not provide medical advice, medical care, or emergency services. Families should continue working with their medical, hospice, oncology, and care teams for health-related decisions. A Moment request is about meaningful time, not medical treatment or medical outcomes.

## You Do Not Have to Wait Until the Very End

Some families assume wish granting for adults with cancer is only for the final days of life. That is not how every Moment needs to work.

A Stage IV cancer wish or late-stage cancer support request may begin while the person still has energy for a short outing, a visit, a meal, a Michigan getaway, or a quiet gathering at home. Waiting too long can make planning harder, especially when travel, fatigue, pain, mobility, privacy, or family schedules become more complicated.

There is no perfect time. But if someone is facing advanced cancer and keeps coming back to an experience, a person, or a place that matters to them, it may be time to reach out.

## Signs It May Be Time to Reach Out

It may be time to request a wish for an adult with cancer when:

- The person has Stage IV, advanced, or metastatic cancer
- The person is thinking about a meaningful experience or gathering
- Family or friends are asking how to help
- Travel, cost, logistics, or planning are becoming barriers
- There is someone they want to see, somewhere they want to go, or something they want to experience
- Hospice, oncology, or social work teams believe a meaningful Moment could be supportive

An oncology referral for an adult cancer wish, a hospice cancer wish referral, or a request from a caregiver can help begin the conversation. The request does not need to be polished or complete. It just needs enough information for One More Moment to understand the situation and follow up with care.

If you are unsure whether someone may qualify, [Who Qualifies for a One More Moment Wish?](/blog/who-qualifies-for-a-one-more-moment-wish/) explains the main considerations in plain language.

## A Moment Does Not Have to Be Large

A Moment does not have to be a big trip, expensive event, or public story.

It may be:

- Dinner with family
- A visit with someone important
- Time at a favorite place
- A short Michigan getaway
- A simple gathering at home
- A quiet day with loved ones
- Help removing a practical barrier so people can be together

Some of the most meaningful Moments are ordinary on the outside. A favorite meal. A familiar view. A porch, a table, a lake, a room full of people who know the person beyond cancer.

If you are wondering what kinds of Moments may be possible, read [What Kind of Wishes Can Adults With Cancer Request?](/blog/what-kind-of-wishes-can-adults-with-cancer-request/).

## For Caregivers, Families, Hospice Workers, and Oncology Teams

Caregivers, family members, hospice workers, oncology teams, and social workers often see needs before anyone says them out loud. They may notice when someone is tired of being only a patient, when a family wants to gather, or when a meaningful experience is becoming difficult because of cost, travel, or planning.

They can help start a request or referral. They can also help provide context if One More Moment needs to understand timing, general health considerations, accessibility, or logistics.

Still, the Moment should center the person facing cancer. The most helpful requests begin with what feels meaningful to them, what they are comfortable with, and who they want involved.

To learn more about the planning process, read [How One More Moment Plans an Adult Cancer Wish in Michigan](/blog/how-one-more-moment-plans-an-adult-cancer-wish-in-michigan/).

## Privacy, Dignity, and Care

One More Moment handles requests with care. A Moment may involve personal information, medical context, family details, and emotional hopes that deserve respect.

Some Moments are shared publicly only with permission. Many stay private. A family should never feel pressure to turn a deeply personal experience into a public story.

You can review the organization's [Privacy Policy](/privacy-policy/) to learn more about how submitted information is used.

## How Requests Are Reviewed

Each request is reviewed thoughtfully because every warrior and family deserves dignity, and every donor deserves to know their gift is used responsibly.

One More Moment considers the person's situation, the requested Moment, mission fit, timing, logistics, safety, available resources, and whether additional information may be needed. Submitting a request does not guarantee approval, funding, timing, or availability. It starts a careful review.

Donations may help cover direct wish-granting expenses such as lodging, meals, transportation, tickets, accessibility needs, or other costs connected to an approved Moment.

Because corporate donors and generous families and supporters help cover overhead, **100% of public donations go directly toward wish granting.**

## Frequently Asked Questions

### When should someone request a wish for an adult with cancer?

Someone should consider requesting a Moment when an adult in Michigan is facing Stage IV, advanced, or metastatic cancer and has a meaningful experience, gathering, visit, or place in mind. Earlier requests can help because planning often takes time.

### Should families wait until hospice before requesting a Moment?

No. Families do not need to wait until hospice before reaching out. Hospice teams may help with a referral when appropriate, but a Moment can be requested earlier if the person is facing late-stage cancer and support is needed.

### Can a caregiver or social worker help start a request?

Yes. A caregiver, family member, friend, hospice worker, oncology team member, or social worker can help begin a request. The Moment should still center the person facing cancer and what feels meaningful to them.

### Does the Moment have to be a large trip or event?

No. A Moment may be a meal, a visit, a favorite place, a short Michigan getaway, a simple gathering, or a quiet day with loved ones. The size of the Moment does not determine its meaning.

### How can someone request a Moment in Michigan?

The best place to begin is the [Request a Moment](/moments/#request-a-moment) form. You can also review [who qualifies](/blog/who-qualifies-for-a-one-more-moment-wish/) or contact One More Moment at [contactus@onemoremoment.org](mailto:contactus@onemoremoment.org) with questions.
